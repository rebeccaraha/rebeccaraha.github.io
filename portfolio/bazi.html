Bazi

Designing for the Internet of Things - Advanced - Carnegie Mellon University | Spring 2017
Independent Project

Scope

Bazi was created with the goal of taking long-distance competitive play out of the purely digital realm and moving it into the physical realm in mind.

This product was developed with the objective of learning how to code a product from scratch - for that reason a lot of user research and user experience considerations were momentarily put on hold to enable growth in terms of technical competency.

Overview of User Interactions

Player One (P1)
Player Two (P2)
P1 picks up their device to indiate that they want to play to P2. P2's device lights up with red, yellow, green and blue lights with an accompanying vibrate to indicate P1 wants to play.
P1's device flashes white to indicate that their request has been sent P2 picks up their device to indicate they want to play as well.
P1's device stops breathing white and vibrates twice to indcaite P2 has entered the game and it is their turn. P2's device breathes white to indicate that it is not their turn but that the game has begun and is still going.
P1 inputs their color pattern and then presses the white button to send it to P2 P2's device stops breathing white, vibrates twice to indicat a pattern is incoming and then flashes the color pattern P2 must play back.
P1's device breathes white to indicate the game is still going. P2 responds with the previous color pattern. If it is completed successfully, their device lights up with a playful white color to indicate they have completed that piece of the game successfully and the respond with a pattern to continue game play. If it is not completed successfully, the device fades a blue to indicate the player has won.
If P1 wins the game, their device flashes all colors of the rainbow to indicate they have won. Otherwise, they receive an indication that the game has continued and they must complete the pattern sent by P2. So on and so fourth...

Wiring Schematic

A schematic of the circuit created with these components is below. This circuit was soldered to a quarter-sized perma protoboard for the final prototype.

This circuit was powered by a rechargble 3.7v Lithium Ion Polymer Battery with a 2500mAh capacity. This LiPo was plugged into a 1000 Rechargable Powerboost which the powered the circuit using a micro-USB cord.

Fabrication

The physical form of Bazi was fabricated from 1/8" milky white acrylic. The shape, a cuboctahedron, was cut from the acrylic using a .dxf file developed in Adobe Illustrator. This lasercut form was hot-glued.

Cuboctahedron Form & Base (Milky White Acrylic)

In addition to the form, button holders were cut in order to ensure the buttons would stay in place when despite the amount of pressure a user would use to press each button. These button holders were thin long rectangles which were cut out of clear acrylic to ensure that the light from the neopixels were still coming through the device clearly and evenly.

Code

For demonstration purposes, a follower and leader device were coded in order to simply the code.

Device 1, the leader, was also coded to use a push-button start as opposed to a hall-effects interrupt as a start in order to test a different interaction with the device.

Device 2, the follower, uses a hall-effects interrupt as planned as a start to verify that interaction as the desired the one.

Next Steps & Iteration

Moving forward a lot of modifications would need to be made and features would need to be added to move Bazi from a concept prototype to a market ready device.

Some of the higher-level necesssary next steps have been outlined below:

  • Add on/off or reset/play again switch.
  • Created a more controlled gameplay where color patterns can only increment by one in length each round.
  • Change form of send button or send action to decrease user confusion and therefore user error.
  • Change battery to a wireless charging battery/base.
